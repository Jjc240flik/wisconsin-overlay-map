## I. Introduction

The wide ranges of land uses in Brown County reflect the diverse demographics,
geography, and communities within the County. From the urban settings of
downtown Green Bay and De Pere to rural towns like Glenmore and Humboldt,
\[Image: Im6\]Brown County is a study in contrasts.

Sometimes as a result of these contrasts, conflicts regarding the use of land can occur. This is
particularly the case as local communities may experience an abrupt shift from primarily rural
communities to more suburban or urban communities as increasing numbers of people call
Brown County home. Communities that were once almost entirely covered by farm fields,
like the Town of Ledgeview and Village of Hobart, have added many new houses and
residents over the last decade.

## II. Goals and Objectives

## Land Use Development Goals

Goal I. Brown County and the county’s communities should work
to manage future growth and development and maintain a
balance between preservation and expansion.

Objective 1. Continue to inventory and update existing land uses in the County
when local communities update comprehensive plans, and periodically analyze
County land use trends.

Objective 3. Provide incentives to developers to use traditional neighborhood
design, conservation subdivisions, and planned developments to promote mixeduse development and preserve greenspace, environmentally sensitive areas, and
agricultural land.

Objective 4. Encourage communities to utilize design standards for commercial,
industrial, and multi-family residential development to promote quality building
and site design.

some interpretation. For instance, in 2000 all lands
owned by the Airport were classified as a transportation
use; however, in 2014 the determination was made to
classify only the physical airport structures (runways,

# III. Land Use Trend Analysis

As identified in Figure 2-1, Brown County
has experienced changes in land uses
over the past 20 years. These trends,
changes, and recommendations how
communities can still achieve orderly,
efficient growth are discussed in this
chapter. This discussion begins with a
look at the existing conditions of the
various land uses. Figure 2-2 maps the
2020 generalized land uses.

## Residential Land Uses

Residential land use is the dominant
category of developed land uses. In
2000, 44,657 acres within the county
were devoted to residential land use,
which was 13 percent of the county’s
land base. Twenty years later,
residential uses have increased by
2,529.3 acres to 47,724 acres of land
(13.9 percent of the county).

Residential land uses in Brown County

Residential land uses in Brown County
are most heavily concentrated within
the Green Bay Metropolitan Area
communities, such as Green Bay,
Allouez, De Pere, Bellevue, Howard, and
Ashwaubenon. Higher rates of
residential growth have occurred in the
communities of Lawrence, Ledgeview,
Suamico and Hobart. Smaller
concentrations also exist in the rural
villages, such as Denmark, Wrightstown,
and Pulaski, and in town hamlets, such
as Greenleaf, Dyckesville, New Franken,
and Hollandtown. Scattered, rural
residential development is also
common across the county.

Older residential areas, typically near
the downtowns of cities and villages,
have a mix of single-family, two-family,
and multi-family structures. As these
municipalities grew, however, these
residential uses tended to become
more separated into their own distinct
areas, creating large tracts of only one
housing type. An example of this
separation of housing types is
noticeable in many communities where
entire blocks (especially along highvolume streets) are uninterrupted blocks
of duplex units. This is also apparent
where large multi-structure apartment
complexes are located adjacent to
one another to provide a “buffer”
between commercial uses and lowerdensity single-family developments.

Although the past trend in these

municipalities has been to separate
housing types, many local
comprehensive plans are now
encouraging some variability in the
housing locations, such as including
duplexes or neighborhood-scale
\[Image: Im1\]
commercial uses on corner lots and
mixing smaller-scale multi-family units
among single-family residences.

Mixing residential types within
neighborhoods has advantages
because it provides various housing
options as individuals pass through their
life cycles and enables people to stay
within a neighborhood (also discussed
in the Living In BC – Housing Element).

Locating smaller multi-family housing
within a neighborhood may also result
in improved maintenance and design
of structures so that they fit into the new
subdivision or existing neighborhood.
