# Washington County – Future Property Development Vision
## TOP COUNTY – Priority Land Research Target

## General Breakdown: Washington County's Vision for Future Property Development

Washington County is one of Wisconsin's most aggressively pro-development counties, positioned directly in the path of Milwaukee's northwest suburban sprawl. The county government actively courts residential and commercial growth through innovative programs like the Next Generation Housing initiative, which targets 1,000 new housing units. The I-41/US-45 corridor serves as the primary growth spine, carrying Milwaukee commuters north into the county's expanding villages and towns.

### Key Growth Corridors
- **I-41/US-45 corridor** (Milwaukee → Germantown → Richfield → Slinger → Hartford → Fond du Lac)
- **Highway 60 corridor** (east-west connector through Jackson and the southern tier)
- **Highway 33 corridor** (east-west through West Bend, connecting to I-41)
- **Highway 175 corridor** (former US 41, parallel to I-41 through Germantown/Slinger)

### 1. Milwaukee Northwest Sprawl — Direct Path
Washington County sits immediately north of Milwaukee and Waukesha counties. As Milwaukee's western and northern suburbs build out, the natural next ring of development is Washington County's southern and eastern municipalities — Germantown, Richfield, Jackson, and the Town of Polk. This is the same dynamic that drove Waukesha County's explosion over the past three decades, now shifting northward.

### 2. Aggressively Pro-Development County
Unlike neighboring Ozaukee County (which preserves farmland with 35-acre minimums), Washington County actively encourages subdivision and residential growth. The county's **Next Generation Housing** program specifically targets creation of 1,000 new residential units. The county government views growth as positive economic development, making rezoning and subdivision approval more favorable than in preservation-focused counties.

### 3. The Oaks of Jackson — Proof of Demand
The Oaks of Jackson, a major residential subdivision, sold out completely by February 2025, demonstrating the intense demand for new housing in the I-41/US-45 corridor. This success has emboldened developers and the county to pursue additional large-scale subdivisions.

### 4. Key Priorities

| Priority | What Planners Want |
|---|---|
| Infrastructure Efficiency | Develop where sewer/water exist along I-41/US-45 corridor |
| Next Generation Housing | 1,000 new housing units via streamlined approval |
| Farmland Preservation Plan 2025 | Balance growth with agricultural preservation (main constraint) |
| Housing Variety | Mix of single-family, multi-family, and workforce housing |
| Economic Growth | Support commercial/industrial along I-41 and Highway 60 |

### 5. Main Constraint: Farmland Preservation Plan 2025
The county's most significant development constraint is its Farmland Preservation Plan, currently being updated in 2025. This plan designates certain agricultural areas (particularly in western townships) for long-term preservation, creating a tension between the county's pro-growth posture and state farmland preservation requirements. The plan update will define where subdivision can and cannot occur for the next decade.

**Bottom Line**
Highest subdivision potential is along the I-41/US-45 corridor in the southern and central portions of the county — Germantown, Richfield, Jackson, Slinger, and the adjacent towns. The county's pro-development stance makes rezoning more achievable than in neighboring Ozaukee or western Waukesha. Western rural townships face more constraints from the Farmland Preservation Plan.

## Summary of Growth Patterns

**Highest Growth Areas (Priority 1):**
Village of Germantown, Village of Jackson, Village of Richfield, Village of Slinger, City of Hartford — I-41/US-45 corridor. Direct Milwaukee NW sprawl path. Next Generation Housing target areas. The Oaks of Jackson demonstrated extreme demand.

**Moderate to High (Priority 2):**
Town of Polk, Town of West Bend, Town of Erin, Town of Hartford, Town of Jackson — Adjacent to HIGH municipalities. FLU shows residential expansion. Spillover from incorporated areas.

**Moderate (Priority 3):**
City of West Bend (county seat infill), Village of Kewaskum (secondary node), Town of Germantown — Stable growth. Some redevelopment and edge expansion.

**Low Growth (Priority 4):**
Western townships (Addison, Farmington, Wayne, Trenton) — Farmland Preservation Plan 2025 constraints. Rural character. Limited sewer/water infrastructure.

## Growth Direction & FLU Analysis

**Growth Direction:** I-41/US-45 corridor NW from Milwaukee into Washington County. **Direct path of least resistance for Milwaukee suburban sprawl.** Primary: Village of Germantown → Village of Richfield → Village of Jackson → Village of Slinger → City of Hartford. Secondary ring: Town of Polk, Town of Erin, Town of West Bend (adjacent to county seat).

### FLU Map Patterns
- **I-41/US-45 Corridor:** Predominantly residential and commercial FLU designations. Villages of Germantown, Richfield, Jackson, and Slinger all show significant residential expansion areas on their future land use maps.
- **Southern Gateway (Germantown/Richfield):** Most intense development pressure. Direct adjacency to Milwaukee/Waukesha county line. Multiple FLU categories supporting mixed residential densities.
- **Central Corridor (Jackson/Slinger/Polk):** Active conversion from agricultural to residential FLU. The Oaks of Jackson development area shows planned residential on former farmland.
- **City of West Bend Area:** FLU shows residential and commercial expansion into surrounding Town of West Bend and Town of Barton. County seat influence.
- **Western Townships:** FLU predominantly agricultural with farmland preservation overlays. Farmland Preservation Plan 2025 update will lock in long-term agricultural designations for Addison, Farmington, Wayne, and Trenton.
- **Kewaskum Corridor (Highway 28):** Secondary growth node west of I-41. Some residential FLU expansion around the village.

### Key Finding
The Washington County government's pro-development posture means FLU maps are more likely to be amended to accommodate growth than in neighboring counties. The Farmland Preservation Plan 2025 is the primary constraint, but its focus is on western townships — leaving the I-41/US-45 corridor largely open for development.

## Next Generation Housing Program

Washington County's signature housing initiative targets **1,000 new residential units** through:
- Streamlined permitting and rezoning processes
- Public-private partnerships with developers
- Infrastructure coordination (sewer/water expansion)
- Mixed-density developments including workforce housing
- Priority zones along the I-41/US-45 corridor

**The Oaks of Jackson** (sold out Feb 2025) is the program's most visible success story, validating the county's pro-growth strategy and attracting additional developer interest.

---

## Farmland Preservation Plan 2025 (Key Constraint)

The Farmland Preservation Plan update (2025) is the primary check on Washington County's otherwise pro-development posture. Key elements:
- Designates Agricultural Enterprise Areas (AEAs) primarily in western townships
- Establishes minimum lot sizes and preservation easements in designated zones
- Creates a tension between county growth goals and state farmland preservation requirements
- Western townships (Addison, Farmington, Wayne, Trenton) most affected
- I-41/US-45 corridor largely exempt from strict preservation designations
