# Kenosha County – Future Property Development Vision
## TOP COUNTY – Priority Land Research Target

## General Breakdown: Kenosha County's Vision for Future Property Development

Kenosha County sits in the southeastern corner of Wisconsin, positioned directly between Chicago and Milwaukee along the I-94 corridor. It is the only Wisconsin county fully within the Chicago metropolitan statistical area. As the #1 hottest US housing market (Realtor.com, January 2026), Kenosha County is experiencing unprecedented growth pressure driven by Illinois-to-Wisconsin migration, Metra commuter rail access to downtown Chicago, and major corporate headquarters expansions.

### 1. Growth Should Be Strategic, Not Scattered
Kenosha County's 2010 Multi-Jurisdictional Comprehensive Plan (adopted April 20, 2010, with SEWRPC assistance) established a framework for directing growth to areas with existing or planned sewer and water infrastructure. The county wants to avoid leapfrog development that consumes productive farmland in western townships.

### 2. The I-94 Chicago-Milwaukee Growth Corridor
The I-94 corridor is the dominant growth axis. Development pressure flows north from Illinois through Pleasant Prairie, Kenosha, and Somers. This corridor benefits from:
- **Metra Union Pacific North Line** — daily commuter rail to downtown Chicago (Kenosha station)
- **I-94** — direct Interstate highway connection
- **Proximity to Mitchell International Airport (Milwaukee)** and **O'Hare International Airport (Chicago)**

### 3. Concentrate Development Around Existing Urban Areas
Most future residential and commercial growth is planned around:
- **Village of Pleasant Prairie** — The economic engine of the county. Massive industrial/commercial growth west of I-94 (Uline HQ, Amazon, Haribo, Nexus Pharmaceuticals). Westward residential expansion.
- **Village of Somers** — Directly north of Kenosha. Being annexed/developed rapidly. UW-Parkside and I-94 visibility.
- **City of Kenosha** — Redevelopment, infill, and downtown revitalization. Harbor district. Limited greenfield.
- **Village of Salem Lakes / Bristol** — Western growth nodes along Highway 50 and Highway 83 corridors.

### 4. Protect Agricultural Land in Western Townships
Western Kenosha County (Brighton, Paris, Wheatland, Randall) remains largely agricultural. Future Land Use maps show these areas staying rural with limited development. Growth should be directed to areas less suitable for farming.

### 5. Support Economic Development & Logistics
Kenosha County is one of the Midwest's premier logistics and distribution hubs:
- **Amazon** — 1 million+ sq ft fulfillment centers
- **Uline** — Global headquarters in Pleasant Prairie (13,000+ employees)
- **Haribo** — First US manufacturing facility
- **Nexus Pharmaceuticals** — State-of-the-art manufacturing
- **Snap-on** — Global HQ in Kenosha
- **Jockey International** — Global HQ in Kenosha

### 6. Each Municipality Controls Its Own Zoning
All zoning decisions are made by individual cities, villages, and towns. The County's role is coordination and planning guidance through the Multi-Jurisdictional Comprehensive Plan.

### 7. Key Priorities for Future Development
| Priority | What Planners Want |
|---|---|
| Infrastructure Efficiency | Develop where sewer, water, and roads already exist |
| I-94 Corridor Focus | Prioritize growth along the Chicago-Milwaukee corridor |
| Agricultural Preservation | Protect farmland in western townships (Brighton, Paris, Wheatland, Randall) |
| Housing Variety | Allow diverse housing types (single-family, multi-family, mixed-use) |
| Economic Growth | Support continued industrial/logistics expansion in Pleasant Prairie/Somers |
| Environmental Protection | Avoid development in wetlands, floodplains, and Lake Michigan coastal areas |

**Bottom Line**
Kenosha County's highest subdivision potential is concentrated in the I-94 corridor villages — Pleasant Prairie (west of I-94 expansion zone) and Somers (rapid annexation/growth). Salem Lakes and Bristol along the Highway 50/83 corridor represent a secondary growth tier. Western townships (Brighton, Paris, Wheatland, Randall) remain agricultural with limited near-term subdivision potential despite the county's overall hot housing market.

## Summary of Growth Patterns

**Highest Growth Areas (Priority 1 — I-94 Corridor & Near Metro):**
Pleasant Prairie, Somers, Bristol, Salem Lakes — These villages are where the strongest development pressure exists. Pleasant Prairie west of I-94 is the county's hottest development zone. Somers is actively transitioning from rural to suburban. Bristol and Salem Lakes are attracting Illinois-to-Wisconsin migration.

**Moderate to High Growth Areas (Priority 2 — Edge-Adjacent to Metro):**
Paris, Wheatland, Randall — Towns on the urban-rural fringe with some development pressure but lacking full sewer/water infrastructure.

**Moderate Growth Areas:**
City of Kenosha, Twin Lakes — Core city (redevelopment/infill focus) and lakeside community with steady but not explosive growth.

**Low to Moderate Growth:**
Paddock Lake, Genoa City — Smaller villages with limited development capacity.

**Low Growth / Agricultural Preservation Areas:**
Brighton — Remains largely agricultural. Strong farmland preservation priority.

**Municipal Reorganization Notes:**
- Silver Lake merged with Salem to form **Salem Lakes** (2017) — now treated as one municipality
- Somers transitioned from Town to Village (2015) — now Village of Somers
- Bristol transitioned from Town to Village (2010) — now Village of Bristol

## Growth Direction & FLU Analysis

**Growth Direction:** Dominant growth axis is the **I-94 Chicago-Milwaukee corridor** flowing NORTH from Illinois through Pleasant Prairie → Kenosha → Somers. Secondary growth corridors:
- **Highway 50 corridor** (E-W): Bristol area, connecting to I-94
- **Highway 83 corridor** (N-S): Salem Lakes to Twin Lakes, connecting western lakes region
- **Highway 31 / Green Bay Road** (N-S): Historic growth spine through Kenosha city

**Direction from Chicago:** Growth flows north from Lake County, Illinois directly into Pleasant Prairie and Kenosha. Metra commuter rail (UP-N line terminus at Kenosha) makes Kenosha County a viable bedroom community for downtown Chicago workers.

**Direction from Milwaukee:** Growth flows south from Racine County into Somers and Kenosha. I-94 provides 35-minute commute to downtown Milwaukee. Some Milwaukee metro spillover into northern Kenosha County.

**Towns in Growth Path (HIGH pressure):**
- Pleasant Prairie — Directly in I-94 path. West of I-94 = explosive industrial + residential growth
- Somers — Directly in I-94 path north of Kenosha. Rapid transition

**Towns with Secondary Growth Path (MODERATE-HIGH pressure):**
- Bristol — Highway 50 corridor near I-94 interchange
- Salem Lakes — Highway 83 corridor, western lakes region
- Paris — Eastern edge adjacent to Kenosha city, I-94 frontage

**Towns NOT in Direct Growth Path (LOW pressure):**
- Brighton — Far west, no major highway corridor
- Wheatland — Far west, limited infrastructure
- Randall — Southwest, limited direct corridor access
