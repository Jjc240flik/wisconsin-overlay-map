# Milwaukee County – Future Growth Direction Analysis
## FLU Map & Comp Plan Research
*2026-07-12 | Sources: SEWRPC VISION 2050, Franklin 2025/2050 CMP, Oak Creek 2040 Comp Plan, Harbor District WaLUP*

---

## OVERALL VERDICT: Primarily Infill/Redevelopment, Not Greenfield

SEWRPC VISION 2050 (regional framework for southeastern Wisconsin) explicitly directs **96% of new households into urban service areas via compact development**. Milwaukee County is the region's most urbanized county. The planning framework at all levels — regional, county, and municipal — prioritizes infill, redevelopment, TOD, and brownfield remediation.

---

## GROWTH DIRECTION BY ZONE

### 1. Overall County Pattern: Infill & Redevelopment Dominant

- **VISION 2050 land use categories for Milwaukee County**: Mixed-Use City Center, Mixed-Use Traditional Neighborhood, Small Lot Traditional Neighborhood — NOT Medium Lot or Large Lot Neighborhood categories at edges
- **96% of new households** directed to existing urban service areas
- **Brownfield remediation** is an explicit VISION 2050 recommendation (Rec. 1.18)
- **Transit-Oriented Development (TOD)** is a major regional priority
- Most suburbs (Wauwatosa, West Allis, Greenfield, Shorewood, Whitefish Bay, etc.) are **fully built out** — infill only

### 2. City of Franklin: THE Greenfield Play in Milwaukee County

**Source: 2025 Comprehensive Master Plan (adopted 2009); 2050 update in progress (2026)**

- **SW portion of Franklin is the county's largest remaining greenfield opportunity**
  - Franklin has a "Pre-Sanitary Sewer Land Use/Zoning, Land Division, and Public Services Policy" (November 2008) specifically for this area
  - The land is zoned but lacks sanitary sewer infrastructure — development gated by utility extension
  - Appendix I of the comp plan is dedicated to this policy
- **2025 Future Land Use Map** (Map 5.7) exists and shows planned growth areas
- The comp plan has **Planning Areas A through I** with detailed subarea plans
- Franklin's vision: "High Quality" development, mixed-use commercial centers, office parks, quality residential
- **63 B+ rezoning-potential parcels** identified in subdivision pipeline (mostly small, scattered)
- **2050 Update**: Partial update in progress (2026), with public participation plan adopted
- Contains agricultural/fallow land in SW that transitions to residential/commercial on FLU map

### 3. City of Oak Creek: Winding Down Greenfield

**Source: 2040 Comprehensive Plan (adopted March 3, 2020)**

- **"Limited land within the City currently remains as actively farmed agricultural land or fallow agricultural land and much of it is either being developed or being marketed for new development"** — direct quote from the plan
- Population: ~36,000 (2017) → projected 40,000+ by 2030
- 28.4 sq miles; 14,485 housing units (49% built since 1990)
- The plan frames the city as navigating a "critical crossroads" toward **becoming a fully built-out city**
- Key emerging developments (mostly redevelopment/infill, not greenfield):
  - **Drexel Town Square** — mixed-use redevelopment of former industrial (Caterpillar site)
  - **Lakeshore Commons** — Lake Michigan waterfront redevelopment
  - **Oakview Business Park** — industrial/commercial
  - **Ryan Business Park** — industrial/commercial
  - **Buc-ee's** — announced at I-94 & Elm Road (early 2027), a greenfield commercial play
- **Lake Michigan coastline**: 4 miles transitioning from obsolete industrial to public space + mixed-use
- **Community concern**: Fast pace of new development, especially distribution/warehousing; desire to preserve remaining land for single-family
- Competitive advantage in Transportation & Warehousing, Manufacturing sectors
- **53 B+ rezoning-potential parcels** identified (mostly small, scattered)

### 4. Harbor District (City of Milwaukee): Brownfield, NOT Greenfield

**Source: Harbor District Water and Land Use Plan (adopted February 6, 2018)**

- **~1,000 acres** of former industrial waterfront at the confluence of Milwaukee's three rivers and Lake Michigan
- **100% brownfield redevelopment** — not greenfield
- Designated as a "catalytic project" in the City's ReFresh Milwaukee sustainability plan
- **Riverwalk**: Design standards adopted May 2020; active extension underway
- **Catalytic projects**:
  - Grand Trunk Wetland restoration (Bay View Wetland)
  - 3.4-acre public riverfront park
  - Floating boardwalk, harbor theater, summer market spaces
  - Shoreline stabilization for projected sea-level rise
- **Mixed-use vision**: Industrial, office, residential, commercial, public space
- **Sub-district plans**: Multiple sub-districts with corridor recommendations
- **Implementation**: Public-private partnership (Harbor District Inc. + BID #51 + City of Milwaukee)
- **Market analysis** included in plan appendices
- This is **NOT a subdivision play** — it's a long-term urban redevelopment play requiring environmental remediation, public infrastructure investment, and phased catalytic projects

## KEY TAKEAWAYS

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| **Infill/redevelopment or greenfield at edges?** | Overwhelmingly infill/redevelopment. SEWRPC directs 96% of growth to existing urban areas |
| **Are Franklin & Oak Creek the only greenfield plays?** | Franklin: YES — SW portion is the county's one real greenfield play, gated by sewer extension. Oak Creek: winding down — limited ag land remaining, plan talks about "fully built-out" transition |
| **What about Harbor District?** | 100% brownfield redevelopment. ~1,000 acres of former industrial waterfront. Catalytic-project model. NOT a subdivision/greenfield play |
| **Where are the real subdivision targets?** | Franklin's SW portion (needs sewer). After that: **Waukesha, Ozaukee, and Washington counties** — the surrounding collar counties with actual greenfield |
